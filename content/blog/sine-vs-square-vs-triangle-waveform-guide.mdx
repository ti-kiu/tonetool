## What's a Waveform?

A waveform is the shape of a sound wave. The same frequency — say, 440 Hz — sounds completely different depending on whether it's a sine wave, square wave, triangle wave, or sawtooth wave.

The difference is in the **harmonics** — extra frequencies that ride along with the fundamental pitch.

## Sine Wave: The Purest Tone

**What it sounds like:** A clean, smooth whistle or flute-like tone. No harshness.

**Harmonics:** None. Just the fundamental frequency.

**When to use it:**

- **Headphone testing** — no extra frequencies to confuse the results
- **Tinnitus matching** — pure pitch, easier to compare to ringing
- **Hearing tests** — isolates one frequency at a time
- **Instrument tuning** — clean reference that's easy to match

**When NOT to use it:**

- Testing speaker distortion (sine waves hide distortion)
- Sound design (too boring)

## Square Wave: Harsh and Buzzy

**What it sounds like:** A buzzy, reedy tone — like a clarinet or old video game sound.

**Harmonics:** Odd harmonics only (3×, 5×, 7× the fundamental). These give it that "hollow" character.

**When to use it:**

- **Testing speaker response** — the harmonics reveal if a speaker can handle high frequencies
- **Checking for distortion** — square waves turn into rounded shapes when a system clips
- **Synthesizer programming** — classic chiptune and bass sound

**Caution:** Square waves contain a lot of energy. At high volumes, they can damage speakers or headphones more easily than sine waves.

## Triangle Wave: Soft and Mellow

**What it sounds like:** A softer, rounder version of a square wave. Less harsh, more "wooden."

**Harmonics:** Odd harmonics like square waves, but they drop off much faster (weaker).

**When to use it:**

- **Gentle testing** — less fatiguing than square waves for long sessions
- **Flute or organ-like sounds** in synthesis
- **When square waves are too harsh** but you still want some harmonic content

## Sawtooth Wave: Bright and Rich

**What it sounds like:** Bright, buzzy, brassy — like a trumpet or string section.

**Harmonics:** All harmonics (odd and even), strong and clear.

**When to use it:**

- **Full-range speaker testing** — covers the entire spectrum in one tone
- **Synthesizer leads and pads** — the richest, most versatile waveform
- **Checking frequency response** — if a system can't reproduce sawtooth harmonics, you'll hear it immediately

## Quick Comparison Table

| Waveform | Harmonics | Sound | Best For |
|----------|-----------|-------|----------|
| Sine | None | Pure, smooth | Tuning, tinnitus, headphone testing |
| Square | Odd only | Buzzy, hollow | Speaker testing, chiptune |
| Triangle | Odd (weak) | Soft, mellow | Gentle testing, flute sounds |
| Sawtooth | All | Bright, brassy | Full-range testing, synthesis |

## Visual Difference

If you could see them:

- **Sine:** Smooth rolling hills
- **Square:** Sharp cliffs (instant on, instant off)
- **Triangle:** Sharp peaks with straight slopes
- **Sawtooth:** Sharp cliff on one side, long slope on the other

## Which One Should You Start With?

For 90% of users: **sine wave**. It's the cleanest, safest, and most precise.

Switch to square or sawtooth only when you're specifically testing equipment response or designing sounds.

## Bottom Line

The waveform changes what you hear without changing the pitch. Sine for precision. Square for testing. Sawtooth for richness. Triangle for something in between.
