## Why Test Your Hearing Range?

Your hearing changes over time. Exposure to loud music, construction noise, or even aging gradually reduces the highest frequencies you can hear.

Knowing your limit helps you:

- **Choose headphones** — no point paying for 40 kHz response if you can't hear above 15 kHz
- **Set realistic expectations** for audio quality
- **Track changes** over time (test monthly)
- **Know when to see a doctor** — sudden drops are a red flag

## What You Need

- A quiet room
- Good headphones or earbuds
- [Tone Generator](https://tonetool.org)
- 5 minutes

## How to Test

### Step 1: Start at 1,000 Hz

Play a 1,000 Hz sine wave at comfortable volume. This is your reference — you should clearly hear this.

### Step 2: Go Up in Steps

Increase frequency in 1,000 Hz steps. Note the highest frequency where you can **clearly** hear the tone.

| Frequency | Typical Hearing Status |
|-----------|----------------------|
| 8,000 Hz | Everyone should hear this |
| 12,000 Hz | Most adults can hear this |
| 15,000 Hz | Many adults start losing this |
| 16,000 Hz | Teenagers usually hear this; many adults don't |
| 17,000 Hz | Young adults only |
| 18,000 Hz | Teenagers and young adults |
| 19,000–20,000 Hz | Children and teenagers |

### Step 3: Fine-Tune Your Limit

Once you find the range where it disappears, narrow it down to the exact frequency:

- If you hear 15,000 Hz but not 16,000 Hz, test 15,500 Hz
- Then 15,200 Hz, 15,400 Hz, etc.

Your limit is the highest frequency you can hear at normal volume.

## What Your Result Means

| Upper Limit | Typical Age Group | Notes |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|
| 18–20 kHz | Under 20 | Normal for youth |
| 15–17 kHz | 20–40 | Normal; some high-frequency loss |
| 12–14 kHz | 40–60 | Common; significant high-frequency loss |
| Under 12 kHz | 60+ | Expected; consider hearing check |

**Important:** These are rough averages. Genetics, noise exposure, and health all play a role. A 30-year-old who attended many concerts might hear less than a 50-year-old who protected their ears.

## Factors That Affect Your Score

- **Headphone quality** — cheap earbuds may not reproduce high frequencies even if your ears can hear them
- **Volume level** — higher volumes make high frequencies easier to hear (don't crank it — hearing damage is permanent)
- **Room noise** — even quiet rooms have ambient sound that masks high frequencies
- **Fatigue** — tired ears hear less
- **Earwax** — blocked ears reduce all frequencies

## Don't Cheat

- Don't turn the volume way up to hear higher frequencies — you're not testing your ears, you're testing your pain tolerance
- Don't use square or sawtooth waves — the harmonics make it easier to "hear" frequencies that are actually just distortion
- Test in a quiet room with good headphones

## When to See a Doctor

- Sudden drop in hearing (overnight or over a few days)
- One ear hears significantly less than the other
- Hearing loss with pain, dizziness, or ringing
- Hearing loss after loud noise exposure
