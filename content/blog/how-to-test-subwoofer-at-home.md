# How to Test Your Subwoofer at Home (Without Expensive Equipment)

A subwoofer is supposed to produce deep bass. But how do you know if it's doing its job? Most people just turn up the volume and assume everything is working. That's not a test — that's just making it loud.

A proper subwoofer test checks three things: frequency response (how low it plays), crossover point (where it hands off to your main speakers), and output level (how loud it gets before distortion). Here's how to do all three with free tools.

## What you need

- A computer or phone with a web browser
- A subwoofer connected and powered on
- A frequency tone generator (free online tools work fine)
- A SPL meter app on your phone (optional but helpful)

You do not need an RTA (real-time analyzer) or a calibrated microphone. Those tools give you precise numbers, but for most home setups, your ears are accurate enough to identify problems.

## Test 1: How low can your subwoofer play?

The most important spec for a subwoofer is its low-frequency extension — the lowest frequency it can reproduce at a useful volume. Most consumer subwoofers are rated to 35 Hz or 40 Hz. Cheap ones might only reach 50 Hz.

**Step 1:** Generate a sine wave tone at 60 Hz. Play it through your subwoofer at moderate volume.

**Step 2:** Lower the frequency in 5 Hz steps: 55 Hz, 50 Hz, 45 Hz, 40 Hz, 35 Hz, 30 Hz, 25 Hz.

**Step 3:** At each frequency, listen carefully. Note when the sound drops off noticeably or disappears.

Here is what most subwoofers can do:

| Frequency | What You Should Hear |
|---|---|
| 60 Hz | Strong, audible bass from any subwoofer |
| 50 Hz | Still loud on most quality subwoofers |
| 45 Hz | Good subwoofers remain strong here |
| 40 Hz | Entry-level subs start to drop off |
| 35 Hz | Only quality 10" or 12" subwoofers reach here |
| 30 Hz | Requires a large, high-quality subwoofer |
| 25 Hz | Only professional or custom subwoofers |
| 20 Hz | The theoretical limit of human hearing |

If your subwoofer drops off at 50 Hz, it's essentially a mid-bass driver, not a true subwoofer. Good subwoofers should play cleanly down to at least 35 Hz.

**One thing to watch:** Below about 30 Hz, you might feel vibration more than hear it. Put your hand on the subwoofer cone or near the port. If you feel movement but hear nothing, the sub is working — you're just below the threshold of hearing.

## Test 2: Finding your crossover point

The crossover point is where your subwoofer stops playing and your main speakers take over. Most home theater systems use an 80 Hz crossover. Music systems sometimes use 60 Hz.

**Step 1:** Set your receiver or amplifier's crossover to 80 Hz (the THX standard).

**Step 2:** Generate tones from 40 Hz to 120 Hz, stepping up in 10 Hz increments.

**Step 3:** At each frequency, note whether you hear the sound coming from the subwoofer or from your main speakers.

**Step 4:** The crossover point is where the sound transitions from the sub to the mains. If there is a gap — a frequency range where neither the sub nor the mains are producing sound — your crossover is set wrong.

**Common crossover issues:**

- **Crossover too high (120 Hz+):** The subwoofer becomes directional. You can hear exactly where it is in the room. Bass should be non-directional, so 80 Hz or below is recommended.
- **Crossover too low (60 Hz):** Your main speakers might not play low enough to meet the subwoofer. This creates a gap in the 60–80 Hz range.
- **Phase mismatch:** If bass sounds hollow or weak at the crossover point, your subwoofer might be out of phase with your main speakers. Most subwoofers have a phase switch (0° or 180°). Flip it and listen again.

## Test 3: Checking for distortion

Distortion in a subwoofer sounds like rattling, buzzing, or a "farting" noise, especially at low frequencies. It means the subwoofer is being pushed beyond its limits.

**Step 1:** Generate a 40 Hz sine wave (this is the hardest frequency for most subwoofers).

**Step 2:** Start at low volume and gradually increase.

**Step 3:** Listen for distortion. You might hear:

- **Cone flutter:** The cone makes a slapping or rattling sound. This means the suspension is too loose or the cone is too flimsy.
- **Port noise:** If your sub has a port (hole or slot), you might hear a chuffing or whistling sound at high volume. This is air turbulence in the port.
- **Cabinet rattle:** The subwoofer box itself vibrates. This usually means the cabinet is not rigid enough or screws are loose.

Note the volume level where distortion starts. This is your subwoofer's maximum clean output at that frequency.

**Typical maximum clean output for consumer subwoofers:**

| Subwoofer Size | Max Clean Output (estimated) |
|---|---|
| 8-inch | 95–100 dB at 1 meter |
| 10-inch | 100–108 dB at 1 meter |
| 12-inch | 105–115 dB at 1 meter |
| 15-inch | 110–120 dB at 1 meter |

These numbers vary widely by build quality and enclosure design.

## Test 4: Room modes and nulls

Room modes are standing waves that cause certain frequencies to be much louder or quieter than others at your listening position. This is the single biggest factor affecting bass quality in a room.

**Step 1:** Sit in your normal listening position.

**Step 2:** Have someone else play tones from 30 Hz to 100 Hz, stepping up in 5 Hz increments.

**Step 3:** At each frequency, rate the volume from 1 (barely audible) to 5 (loud).

**Step 4:** Plot the results. You will likely see peaks (modes) and dips (nulls). A 10 dB dip at a specific frequency means that frequency is being canceled by room reflections.

**How to fix room modes:**

- Move the subwoofer to a different position. Corner placement maximizes output but also maximizes room modes.
- Use a second subwoofer on the opposite side of the room to even out the response.
- Add bass traps (thick acoustic panels) in the corners of the room.

## FAQ

### What Hz should I test my subwoofer at?

Test at 30 Hz, 40 Hz, 50 Hz, 60 Hz, and 80 Hz. These five frequencies cover the critical range for most subwoofers. 40 Hz is the single most demanding test frequency for a typical home subwoofer.

### How do I know if my subwoofer is working?

Play a 60 Hz sine wave at moderate volume. If you hear or feel deep bass from the subwoofer, it's working. If you hear rattling, buzzing, or nothing at all, there may be a connection or amplifier issue.

### What is the ideal crossover frequency for a subwoofer?

80 Hz is the THX-recommended crossover for home theater. For music, 60 Hz works well if your main speakers can play that low. Never set the crossover above 120 Hz — bass becomes directional above that point.

### How low should a good subwoofer play?

A good subwoofer should reach at least 35 Hz at a useful volume. Budget subwoofers often drop off at 40–50 Hz. High-quality 12-inch subwoofers can play down to 25 Hz or lower.
