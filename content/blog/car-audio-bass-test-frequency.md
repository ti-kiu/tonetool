# Car Audio Bass Test: What Frequency to Use for Your Subwoofer

Most car subwoofers are rated to play down to 30 or 35 Hz. But rated and actual are different things. A subwoofer that specss 30 Hz on the box might start distorting at 35 Hz in a real car trunk. The only way to know what your setup actually does is to test it with specific frequencies.

This guide tells you exactly which frequencies to use for each type of car audio test, and what problems each one reveals.

## Why car audio is different from home audio

A car cabin is small and irregularly shaped. This creates standing waves that do not exist in a typical room. A 50 Hz tone in a sedan can be 15 dB louder in the back seat than at the driver's head. This means your subwoofer sounds different depending on where you sit.

Car subwoofers also face different constraints than home subwoofers:

- **Space is limited.** Most car subs are 10 or 12 inches in a sealed or ported box that fits in a trunk corner.
- **Power is limited.** A typical car amplifier puts out 300 to 500 watts RMS. Home subwoofers regularly handle 1,000 watts.
- **Electrical noise is high.** The alternator, ignition system, and wiring create noise that masks low-level bass detail.

These factors mean you need to test differently than you would at home.

## Test 1: Low-frequency extension (30 Hz to 60 Hz)

This test finds the lowest frequency your subwoofer can play at a useful volume.

**Step 1:** Set your head unit volume to about 75% of maximum. Turn off bass boost and any EQ.

**Step 2:** Generate a 60 Hz sine wave. Play it for 10 seconds. This is your baseline. It should be loud and clean.

**Step 3:** Drop to 50 Hz. Listen for volume loss.

**Step 4:** Drop to 45 Hz. Listen carefully.

**Step 5:** Drop to 40 Hz. This is the critical frequency for most car subwoofers.

**Step 6:** Drop to 35 Hz and then 30 Hz.

Here is what to expect from common car subwoofer sizes:

| Sub Size | Typical Low Extension | What Happens Below |
|---|---|---|
| 8-inch sealed | 40–45 Hz | Rapid volume loss, no output below 35 Hz |
| 10-inch sealed | 35–40 Hz | Usable to 35 Hz, weak below |
| 10-inch ported | 30–35 Hz | Good output to 30 Hz |
| 12-inch sealed | 32–38 Hz | Solid to 35 Hz, usable to 30 Hz |
| 12-inch ported | 28–32 Hz | Strong output to 30 Hz |
| 15-inch ported | 25–30 Hz | Deep bass below 30 Hz |

If your 12-inch subwoofer has no audible output at 35 Hz, something is wrong. Either the box is too small, the port is tuned too high, or the subwoofer itself has a high resonant frequency (Fs).

## Test 2: Distortion check (40 Hz)

Forty hertz is the hardest frequency for most car subwoofers. It sits right at the low end of their usable range, where the cone has to move the most air. This is where distortion shows up first.

**Step 1:** Generate a 40 Hz sine wave.

**Step 2:** Start at low volume (about 30% on your head unit).

**Step 3:** Slowly increase volume over 30 seconds.

**Step 4:** Listen for these problems:

- **Buzzing or rattling:** Something in the trunk is loose. Check the license plate, spare tire, trunk liner, and any loose objects.
- **Cone bottoming out:** A hard thumping or slapping sound. The subwoofer cone is hitting the back plate. This means the sub is being overdriven or the box is too small.
- **Port chuffing:** A whistling or chuffing noise from the port. Air is moving too fast through the port. You need a larger port or a different box design.
- **Amplifier clipping:** The bass sounds distorted and harsh, not just loud. Your amplifier is being pushed beyond its clean output. Turn it down or get a more powerful amp.

Note the volume level where distortion starts. Back it off 10% from that point. That is your maximum clean output level.

## Test 3: Phase alignment with main speakers (60 Hz to 120 Hz)

The crossover between your subwoofer and your door speakers determines whether bass sounds punchy or hollow. If the sub and the doors are out of phase at the crossover point, bass cancels out and sounds thin.

**Step 1:** Set your crossover to 80 Hz (the standard for most car audio systems).

**Step 2:** Play an 80 Hz tone.

**Step 3:** Have someone flip the phase switch on your amplifier (0 degrees vs 180 degrees) while you listen from the driver's seat.

**Step 4:** Choose the setting that sounds louder and fuller at 80 Hz. Louder means the sub and the doors are in phase (working together). Quieter means they are out of phase (canceling each other).

**Step 5:** If your amplifier does not have a phase switch, you can achieve the same effect by reversing the wire polarity on the subwoofer (swap the + and - wires at the amp or at the sub).

A properly phased system at 80 Hz should sound seamless. You should not be able to tell where the bass is coming from.

## Test 4: Cabin resonances (40 Hz to 80 Hz sweep)

Car cabins have strong resonances that make some frequencies much louder than others. This is the main reason car audio sounds different from home audio.

**Step 1:** Set your head unit to a moderate volume.

**Step 2:** Play a slow frequency sweep from 40 Hz to 80 Hz over 30 seconds.

**Step 3:** Listen for frequency ranges that suddenly get much louder (peaks) or much quieter (nulls).

Common sedan resonance frequencies:

| Frequency | Typical Behavior |
|---|---|
| 45–55 Hz | Strong peak in most sedans (cabin gain) |
| 60–70 Hz | Often a null between front and rear seats |
| 75–80 Hz | Transition to door speakers, may sound thin |

**How to fix resonances:**
- Move the subwoofer position. Under a seat, in the trunk corner, or in the center of the trunk each produce different resonance patterns.
- Use a parametric EQ to cut the peak frequency by 3 to 6 dB.
- A second subwoofer on the opposite side of the trunk evens out the response.

## FAQ

### What Hz is best for testing a car subwoofer?

Use 40 Hz for distortion testing (the hardest frequency for most car subs), 60 Hz for phase alignment with door speakers, and 30 Hz to test low-frequency extension. Run a sweep from 30 to 80 Hz to find cabin resonances.

### How do I know if my car subwoofer is working properly?

Play a 60 Hz sine wave at moderate volume. You should hear strong, clean bass. If you hear rattling, buzzing, or the bass sounds thin, there is a problem with the sub, the box, or the wiring.

### What frequency should I set my car subwoofer crossover to?

80 Hz is the standard crossover point for most car audio systems. Set your head unit or amplifier high-pass filter on the door speakers to 80 Hz, and the low-pass filter on the subwoofer to 80 Hz. This prevents the subwoofer from playing midrange and the doors from trying to play deep bass.

### Why does my car bass sound different from my home bass?

A car cabin creates strong standing waves and resonances that do not exist in a typical room. The small, irregular space amplifies certain frequencies and cancels others. This is why car audio needs equalization and careful subwoofer placement.
