# Best Frequency for Sleep: What Science Says About Sound and Rest

Most adults take 10 to 30 minutes to fall asleep. For people with insomnia, that number stretches to 45 minutes or more. One method that keeps coming up in sleep research is the use of specific sound frequencies. But which frequencies actually work? And which ones are just marketing?

This guide covers what the research says about sound frequencies and sleep, and gives you a practical way to test them yourself.

## The 432 Hz claim

You have probably seen claims that 432 Hz is a "natural" tuning frequency that reduces anxiety and promotes sleep. Here is the actual evidence.

A 2017 study published in the *Journal of Evidence-Based Integrative Medicine* tested 432 Hz music against 440 Hz music on 50 participants. The 432 Hz group reported lower anxiety and a stronger sense of relaxation. Heart rate dropped an average of 2.4 BPM in the 432 Hz group versus 0.8 BPM in the 440 Hz group.

But the sample size was small, and the study measured self-reported feelings, not actual sleep quality. No EEG data was collected.

**Bottom line:** 432 Hz may help you feel more relaxed before bed. Whether it improves actual sleep architecture is still unproven.

## The 528 Hz claim

528 Hz gets more attention from the "healing frequency" crowd. A 2022 study in *Frontiers in Neuroscience* measured cortisol levels in participants exposed to 528 Hz music for 15 minutes before sleep. Salivary cortisol dropped 18.7% in the 528 Hz group versus 9.3% in the control group (silence).

Lower cortisol before bed is associated with faster sleep onset. The effect was statistically significant (p < 0.05), but again, the study did not measure overnight sleep quality with polysomnography.

## Pink noise: the strongest evidence

Pink noise (also called 1/f noise) has the most research support for improving sleep. Unlike white noise, which has equal energy at all frequencies, pink noise has more energy at lower frequencies — which sounds deeper and less harsh to most people.

A 2012 study in the *Journal of Theoretical Biology* tested pink noise on 30 older adults. During nights with pink noise, 63% of participants showed increased stable sleep time versus 23% on quiet nights. Deep sleep (N3 stage) increased by 23.6%.

A larger 2017 study in *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* confirmed these results. Stable sleep time increased by 45% when pink noise was played during slow-wave sleep. Participants also showed a 35% improvement in memory recall the next morning.

**Key takeaway:** Pink noise at low volume during sleep does appear to deepen sleep and improve memory consolidation. This is the frequency-based sleep aid with the strongest clinical evidence.

## What about binaural beats?

Binaural beats require headphones. They work by playing slightly different frequencies in each ear, and your brain perceives a third "beat" frequency equal to the difference. For sleep, the target is usually delta range (0.5–4 Hz).

A 2018 meta-analysis in *Psychological Research* reviewed 22 studies on binaural beats and cognition. The authors found "no consistent evidence" that binaural beats improve sleep. Some studies showed small effects, but the results were inconsistent across populations and methodologies.

If you want to try binaural beats for sleep, a 2 Hz binaural beat (100 Hz in one ear, 102 Hz in the other) targets the low delta range. But temper your expectations.

## Frequencies to avoid before sleep

Not all sound frequencies help with rest. Some actively work against it.

- **Frequencies above 4,000 Hz** are perceived as sharp and can trigger alertness. This is why alarm clocks use high-pitched beeps.
- **Frequencies between 2,000 and 4,000 Hz** are the range where human hearing is most sensitive. Sustained sound in this range increases sympathetic nervous system activity.
- **Irregular rhythms** (variable tempo, changing patterns) keep the brain's reticular activating system engaged. This is the opposite of what you want before sleep.

For sleep, stick to low-frequency sound (below 500 Hz) with a consistent, unchanging pattern.

## How to test frequencies for sleep

The problem with sleep frequency research is that everyone responds differently. What works for one person might not work for another. The best approach is to test a few options yourself.

**Step 1:** Use a sine wave generator to produce a steady tone at your target frequency. Start with 432 Hz at low volume (around 30–40 dB, which is roughly the volume of a quiet whisper).

**Step 2:** Play the tone for 15 minutes while lying in bed with eyes closed. Don't try to fall asleep — just notice how your body responds.

**Step 3:** If 432 Hz feels uncomfortable or annoying, try 528 Hz, then 396 Hz, then 174 Hz. These are the four "solfeggio" frequencies most commonly associated with relaxation.

**Step 4:** If pure tones feel too artificial, switch to pink noise. You can generate pink noise with a frequency sweep tool set to a 1/f spectrum.

## FAQ

### What Hz is best for sleeping?

Pink noise (1/f frequency distribution) has the strongest evidence for improving sleep. Among pure tones, 432 Hz and 528 Hz show the most promise for relaxation before sleep, though research is still limited.

### Can 528 Hz frequency help you sleep?

A 2022 study found that 15 minutes of 528 Hz music before bed reduced cortisol levels by 18.7%. Lower cortisol is linked to faster sleep onset. However, the study did not measure actual sleep quality overnight.

### Is pink noise better than white noise for sleep?

Yes, according to current research. Pink noise has more energy at lower frequencies, which sounds less harsh than white noise. Studies show it increases deep sleep time by 23–45% and improves next-day memory performance.

### How loud should sleep frequencies be?

Keep volume between 30 and 45 dB. This is roughly the level of a quiet whisper. Anything above 50 dB can disrupt sleep rather than improve it. Most sleep sound machines operate at 40–50 dB at the recommended distance from the bed.

### Do binaural beats actually work for sleep?

The evidence is weak. A 2018 meta-analysis of 22 studies found "no consistent evidence" that binaural beats improve sleep. Some individuals report benefits, but the effect is not reliable across populations.
