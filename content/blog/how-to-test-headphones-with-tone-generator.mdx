## Why Test Your Headphones?

You just bought new headphones. They sound "off" — but you can't tell if it's the bass, mids, or highs. Or maybe one ear sounds quieter than the other. Before you return them, run a quick frequency sweep. It takes 2 minutes and tells you exactly what's wrong.

## What You Need

- Your headphones
- A device with a browser (phone, tablet, or computer)
- [Tone Generator](https://tonetool.org) — free, no signup

## Step 1: Check for Dead Drivers

Play a **1,000 Hz sine wave** at moderate volume. You should hear a clean, steady tone in both ears.

- If one ear is silent → that driver is dead
- If one ear is noticeably quieter → driver imbalance or connection issue

> **Tip:** Start at low volume and increase gradually. A 1kHz tone at high volume can be uncomfortable.

## Step 2: Test Bass Response (20–250 Hz)

Headphones vary wildly in bass. Some boost it. Others roll off early.

| Frequency | What to Listen For |
|-----------|-------------------|
| 20–40 Hz | Sub-bass rumble. Many headphones can't reproduce this. |
| 60–100 Hz | Kick drum territory. Should feel punchy, not muddy. |
| 125–250 Hz | Bass guitar body. Too much = boomy; too little = thin. |

Play each frequency for 5–10 seconds. Note where the sound drops off or becomes distorted.

## Step 3: Check Mids and Highs (250 Hz–8 kHz)

This is where vocals, guitars, and most instruments live.

- **500 Hz–2 kHz:** Vocal clarity. A dip here makes voices sound distant.
- **4 kHz–8 kHz:** Presence and detail. Too much = harsh; too little = dull.

## Step 4: Test Treble Extension (8–20 kHz)

Not everyone can hear above 15 kHz (age and hearing damage reduce this). But if you *can* hear:

- **8–12 kHz:** Cymbals, hi-hats, "air" in recordings
- **12–16 kHz:** Extreme detail — some headphones roll off here
- **16–20 kHz:** Barely audible for most adults. If you hear nothing, it's usually your ears, not the headphones.

## Step 5: Listen for Distortion and Buzzing

Play a **sine wave at 440 Hz** and slowly increase volume. The tone should stay clean. If you hear:

- **Buzzing or rattling** → driver defect or loose component
- **Crackling** → blown driver or damaged voice coil
- **Static** → poor connection or cable issue

## Step 6: Compare Left and Right

Play the same frequency in each ear separately. Most people have slight hearing differences between ears, but a large volume difference (more than 3 dB) suggests a headphone issue.

## What If You Find a Problem?

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Action |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| One ear silent | Dead driver | Return/replace |
| Bass missing below 60 Hz | Design limitation | Normal for on-ears; consider over-ears |
| Harsh highs | Treble peak at 6–8 kHz | Try EQ or different headphones |
| Muddy bass | Excessive 100–200 Hz boost | Try EQ reduction |
| Intermittent sound | Cable/connection issue | Check plug and cable |

## Quick Test Routine (2 Minutes)

1. 1,000 Hz — both ears, check balance
2. 50 Hz — bass extension
3. 440 Hz — distortion check
4. 8,000 Hz — treble clarity
5. Sweep from 20 Hz to 20 kHz — note dropouts
