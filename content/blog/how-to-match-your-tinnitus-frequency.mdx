## Why Match Your Tinnitus Frequency?

Your doctor asks: "What pitch is your ringing?" You shrug. You can't describe a sound.

But if you know the exact frequency — say, 7,500 Hz — your audiologist can test that specific range, check for hearing loss at that pitch, and tailor treatment. It's one number that changes the conversation.

## What You Need

- A quiet room
- Good headphones or earbuds (you need to hear high frequencies clearly)
- [Tone Generator](https://tonetool.org) — free, works in your browser
- 10–15 minutes of patience

## Step 1: Start Broad

Tinnitus usually falls between **3,000 Hz and 12,000 Hz**. Start at **6,000 Hz** and play a sine wave at low-to-moderate volume.

- If the tone sounds *lower* than your tinnitus → go higher
- If it sounds *higher* → go lower

## Step 2: Narrow It Down

Use the slider or type values directly. Move in **1,000 Hz steps** first:

- 4,000 Hz → 5,000 Hz → 6,000 Hz → 7,000 Hz → 8,000 Hz

Once you're within 1,000 Hz, switch to **100 Hz steps**:

- 7,000 Hz → 7,100 Hz → 7,200 Hz → ... → 7,800 Hz

Then use **10 Hz steps** for the final match.

## Step 3: The "Match" Moment

You'll know you found it when the external tone and your internal ringing **blend together** — they sound like one sound. If you can still distinguish two separate tones, you're close but not there yet.

## Step 4: Verify Both Ears

Tinnitus can be different in each ear. Test one ear at a time:

1. Cover one ear or remove one earbud
2. Match the frequency for the open ear
3. Note the Hz value
4. Switch ears and repeat

## Common Tinnitus Frequency Ranges

| Type | Typical Frequency | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| High-pitched ringing | 6,000–12,000 Hz | Most common; often noise-induced |
| Low humming | 125–500 Hz | Less common; check for vascular causes |
| Multi-tone | Multiple frequencies | More complex; note all that apply |
| Pulsatile | Rhythmic, matches heartbeat | Not frequency-based; see a doctor |

## What to Tell Your Doctor

Write down:

1. **Frequency** (e.g., "7,500 Hz")
2. **Ear(s)** affected (left, right, or both)
3. **When it started** and any triggers (loud noise, infection, medication)
4. **Whether it's constant or intermittent**

## Important: What This Tool Can't Do

- **It cannot diagnose** hearing loss, Meniere's disease, or any medical condition
- **It cannot treat** tinnitus
- **It cannot measure** the loudness of your tinnitus (only the pitch)

This tool helps you **communicate** with your doctor. The diagnosis and treatment are their job.

## When to See a Doctor Immediately

- Tinnitus after a loud noise exposure (concert, explosion)
- Tinnitus with sudden hearing loss
- Tinnitus with dizziness or vertigo
- Tinnitus in only one ear that started suddenly
- Pulsatile tinnitus (rhythmic, matches heartbeat)

## Bottom Line

Matching your tinnitus frequency takes 10 minutes and gives you a concrete number to bring to your appointment. It's not a diagnosis — it's a starting point for a better conversation with your doctor.
