# How to Test Speakers with a Frequency Sweep

A frequency sweep plays every frequency from low to high. It goes from 20 Hz (deep bass) up to 20,000 Hz (high treble) and back down. This is one of the simplest ways to test speakers, headphones, or any audio equipment.

When you play a frequency sweep through your speakers, you can hear problems that you might not notice with music. Rattles, dead spots, distortion, and frequency response problems all become obvious.

## What to listen for during a frequency sweep

**Rattles and vibrations:** If you hear buzzing or rattling at certain frequencies, something is loose. It could be the speaker cabinet, a screw, or an object near the speaker that's vibrating.

**Dead spots:** Some frequencies might suddenly get very quiet. This could mean the speaker has a frequency response problem, or there's a room mode canceling that frequency.

**Harshness:** If certain frequencies are much louder than others, the speaker might have a peak in its frequency response. This is common with cheap speakers.

**Bass drop-off:** Good speakers produce audible sound down to about 50 Hz. If your speakers stop being audible around 100 Hz, they have limited bass response.

## How to do a frequency sweep test

**Step 1:** Set the volume to a moderate level. Don't start at maximum — some frequencies can be damaging at high volumes.

**Step 2:** Play the sweep. Listen carefully from start (20 Hz) to finish (20,000 Hz).

**Step 3:** Note any frequencies where you hear problems. Write them down if needed.

**Step 4:** If you find a problem frequency, play just that frequency to confirm.

**Step 5:** Move your head around (or move the speaker) and test again. Some problems are related to position.

## Frequency ranges and what they do

**20-60 Hz:** Deep bass, felt more than heard. Speaker can't reproduce it, cabinet rattles.

**60-200 Hz:** Bass, kick drums. Boomy or muddy sound.

**200-2000 Hz:** Midrange, vocals, instruments. Harshness, boxy sound.

**2000-6000 Hz:** Presence, clarity. Sibilance (harsh "s" sounds).

**6000-20000 Hz:** Air, brightness, detail. Harshness, hiss.

## Testing headphones vs speakers

The test is similar, but there are differences:

**Headphones:**
- Room acoustics don't matter
- You can hear much lower frequencies (good headphones go down to 20 Hz)
- The test reveals problems with the headphone drivers themselves

**Speakers:**
- Room acoustics affect what you hear — bass can be boosted or canceled by room modes
- Try the test in different positions in the room
- Place speakers at ear height for the most accurate test

## What is a frequency response test?

Frequency response measures how accurately a speaker reproduces all frequencies. A perfect speaker would play every frequency at the same volume. In reality, speakers have peaks and dips in their response.

The human ear can hear from about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Good speakers should cover this range fairly evenly, with maybe ±3 dB variation. Cheap speakers might have ±10 dB or more variation, which means some frequencies are much louder than others.

## Tips for accurate testing

1. **Test at moderate volume.** Too loud and you might damage your speakers or ears. Too quiet and you won't hear subtle problems.

2. **Test in a quiet room.** Background noise masks problems in the sweep.

3. **Close your eyes.** It helps you focus on what you're hearing.

4. **Test both speakers.** If one speaker sounds different from the other at certain frequencies, something is wrong with one of them.

5. **Test at your listening position.** The sound at your desk might be different from the sound near the speaker.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I test if my speakers are working?

Play a frequency sweep from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. You should hear sound throughout the entire range. If certain frequencies are silent or very quiet, your speakers might have a problem. Also try playing pink noise — you should hear a balanced, consistent sound.

### What is a good frequency response for speakers?

For most uses, look for speakers that can reproduce at least 60 Hz to 18,000 Hz. Studio monitors should cover 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. The response should be fairly flat — within ±3 dB across the usable range.

### Why do my speakers sound different at different frequencies?

All speakers have some variation in frequency response. Some frequencies are naturally louder or quieter. Room acoustics also play a big role — bass frequencies can be boosted or canceled by room modes, and reflections can cause peaks and dips in the midrange.

### How loud should I test speakers?

Start at about 50-60 dB (moderate conversation level). This is loud enough to hear problems but unlikely to damage your speakers or hearing. Only increase volume if you're testing maximum output.

### Can I test speakers with music instead of a sweep?

Music is good for a general impression, but a frequency sweep is more thorough. Music doesn't play every frequency at a consistent level, so it can mask problems. Use a sweep for diagnostic testing, music for normal listening evaluation.
